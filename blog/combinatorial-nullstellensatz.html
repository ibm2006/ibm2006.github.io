Combinatorial Nullstellensatz

Translated and reformatted from the original notes

Reference: Alon's paper. This post may be updated later.

Skipping a long introduction, I will directly explain what the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz is.

Combinatorial Nullstellensatz

Theorem (Combinatorial Nullstellensatz). Let $F$ be a field, and let $f\in F[x_1,x_2,\cdots , x_n]$ be a polynomial of degree $t_1+\cdots+t_n$. Suppose that $S_1,S_2,\cdots,S_n$ are nonempty subsets of $F$, $|S_i|\ge t_i+1$, and $f[x_1^{t_1}x_2^{t_2}\cdots x_n^{t_n}]\neq 0$. Then there exist $s_1\in S_1,s_2\in S_2,\cdots,s_n\in S_n$ such that $f(s_1,s_2,\cdots,s_n)\neq 0$.

In simple terms, the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz can be interpreted as an extended form of a theorem we know at a basic level: the fundamental theorem of algebra.

This post will not explain the proof of the theorem. The proof may feel tedious and pedantic, and the purpose of this post is to explain the applicability of the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz.

Applications

Let us immediately look at one impressive application.

Problem (Russia MO 2007/5)

For even $n$, consider the cycle $C_n$ with $n$ vertices. At each vertex $v$ of this graph, exactly two numbers among $1,2,\cdots,m$ are written. Show that one can erase one of the two numbers written at each vertex $v$ so that any two adjacent vertices have different remaining numbers.

Of course, there may be several ways to solve this problem. One of them is to apply the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz.

Consider the following polynomial in $n$ variables:

$$f(X)=\prod_{e=uv\in E}(x_u-x_v).$$

It is not hard to show that the coefficient of $x_1x_2\cdots x_n$ in this polynomial is not $0$, and it is also not hard to see that the highest degree of this polynomial is $n$.

If $S_i$ is the set of the two numbers written at the $i$-th vertex, then $|S_i|\ge 2=1+t_i$. Therefore, by the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz, we can choose $s_i\in S_i$ so that $$f(s_1,\cdots,s_n)\neq 0.$$

This means that if we leave $s_1,\cdots,s_n$, then for every $e=uv\in E$ we have $x_u-x_v\neq 0$, so the condition in the problem is satisfied.

In fact, the above problem is equivalent to the $2$-choosability of even cycles. From this point of view, we can see that the Combinatorial Nullstellensatz is extremely useful for solving graph coloring problems.

Let us check one more example.

Problem (IMO 2007/6)

Let $n$ be a positive integer. Consider the set of points in three-dimensional space $$S=\{(x,y,z)\mid x,y,z\in\{0,1,\cdots,n\},\ (x,y,z)\neq(0,0,0)\}.$$ What is the minimum number of planes needed to cover all points of $S$ while none of the planes passes through $(0,0,0)$?

Recall that, in general, a plane can be written in the form $ax+by+cz-d=0$. Suppose that $3n-1$ such planes cover the whole set, and write the $i$-th plane as $a_ix+b_iy+c_iz-d=0$.

Consider the following polynomial:

$$P(x,y,z)=\prod_{i=1}^{3n-1}(a_ix+b_iy+c_iz-d).$$

It is clear that the maximum degree of this polynomial is at most $3n-1$. All points of $S$ must be zeros of this polynomial, while $(0,0,0)$ must not be a zero.

By the grid vanishing lemma in the exercises, this is impossible.

It is clear that $3n$ planes can cover $S$ without covering $(0,0,0)$.

Exercises

  1. Grid vanishing lemma. Let $A_1,\cdots,A_k$ be finite subsets of some field, and let $P(x_1,\cdots,x_k)$ be a polynomial. If $\deg P<\sum_{i=1}^k(|A_i|-1)$, then it is impossible for $P$ to be nonzero at exactly one point of $A_1\times\cdots\times A_k$.
  2. If $A,B$ are subsets of $\mathbb Z_p$, then $$|A+B|\ge \min(p,|A|+|B|-1).$$
  3. Erdos-Heilbronn Conjecture. Let $A$ be a subset of $\mathbb Z_p$. Then $$|\{x+y\mid x,y\in A,x\neq y\}|\ge \min(p,2|A|-3).$$
  4. China 2026 TST. Let $n>2m>2$ be positive integers. There are $n$ people, and some pairs among them are friends. We want to form $m$ committees from these people so that each person belongs to exactly one committee and all people inside each committee are pairwise friends. First, from the $n$ people, choose two representatives for each committee. After doing so, suppose there is exactly one way to assign the remaining $n-2m$ people appropriately to the committees. What is the maximum possible number of friendship pairs for which this situation can occur?
  5. Alon? For any prime $p$, every simple graph whose average degree is greater than $2p-2$ and whose maximum degree is at most $2p-1$ has a $p$-regular subgraph.
